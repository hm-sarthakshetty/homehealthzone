The Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow is a niche machine: a 5 LPM concentrator with two simultaneous oxygen outlets, intended to supply two patients from a single unit. At ₹57,600 per the manufacturer brochure and e-commerce product listings, it fills a specific market slot — lower-cost dual-patient supply without the need for a 10 LPM high-flow unit and Y-split. The design concept is defensible; the execution, like its sibling Niscomed 10 LPM, is not. The spec sheet is almost entirely blank on compliance and alarms, and the stock status is marked Discontinued on the primary listing, suggesting the unit has been phased out. For Indian buyers considering dual-patient home oxygen setups, better alternatives exist.

## What the specs actually mean in Indian homes

The Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow delivers 90-95% oxygen purity at a continuous flow range of 1 to 5 LPM [CITATION: Niscomed Dual Flow product brochure]. The "Dual Flow" designation refers to the unit having two physical oxygen outlets, each capable of delivering up to 5 LPM. In practice, the total concentrator output remains 5 LPM — this is the sum of the two outlets, not 5 LPM each. If both patients need 2.5 LPM, the machine can supply both simultaneously; if one patient needs 4 LPM, the second patient is limited to roughly 1 LPM. This is the same arithmetic as a Y-split on any standard 5 LPM unit, with the difference that the dual-outlet design has independent flow meters on each output, making titration more precise.

Weight is 25 kg. This is very heavy for a 5 LPM — the Keyhub 5 is 16 kg, the Fitmate 5 is 18 kg, the Eloxy is 19 kg. The 25 kg weight reflects the dual-outlet plumbing and the higher-capacity compressor needed to support two simultaneous flow meters. For a fixed installation in a shared bedroom (e.g. an elderly couple both on oxygen) this is a one-time setup issue. For any other scenario, 25 kg is impractical.

Power consumption of 530 W is high for a 5 LPM output — closer to the 10 LPM Niscomed's 530 W than a typical 5 LPM's 300-390 W. This reflects the internal compressor's overbuilt capacity for dual-patient operation. At Indian domestic tariff of ₹8-10 per kWh, 530 W continuous is ₹102-128 per day or ₹37,000-46,000 per year at 24/7 use — higher running cost than many 10 LPM units. Over a 3-year service life at 18 hours/day the electricity bill hits ₹65,000-85,000, exceeding the purchase price.

The cost arithmetic here is worth pausing on: for a dual-patient home installation running 18 hours/day, three years of Niscomed Dual Flow operation costs roughly ₹57,600 (purchase) + ₹75,000 (electricity) + ₹5,000-8,000 (stabiliser) + ₹15,000-25,000 (2 years service/maintenance) = ₹152,600-165,600 total. Alternatively, two separate Keyhub 5 LPM units (total ₹84,480 purchase + roughly ₹45,000 electricity for combined 640 W + ₹7,000 stabilisers + ₹20,000 service) = ₹156,480 total. The dual-flow Niscomed does not meaningfully save money compared to two separate units, and it introduces single-point-of-failure risk (one unit breaks, both patients lose oxygen).

Noise at 50 dB is acceptable for living-room daytime use, not acceptable for bedroom night use.

Outlet pressure of 10 psi is strong for dual-cannula setups where each branch needs adequate pressure. This is one of the Niscomed Dual Flow's genuine advantages over a Y-split on a lower-pressure unit.

Dimensions of 23.6 × 14.7 × 14.3 inches are standard.

Compliance: ALL rows blank. US FDA, FAA, CE all blank. Loss of Power, System Malfunction, No Flow alarms all blank [CITATION: Niscomed Dual Flow spec sheet]. Same pattern as Niscomed 10 LPM — a machine with zero declared safety documentation.

## Who should buy the Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow

Almost no one. The defensible profile is a specific edge case: an elderly couple both on stable low-flow oxygen (each 1-2 LPM), living in a shared bedroom, with a family caregiver who is continuously present, where the single-machine convenience of two outlets outweighs the single-point-of-failure risk. In that scenario the dual-flow architecture is marginally more practical than a Y-split on a standard 5 LPM. Even in that scenario, buying two separate budget 5 LPM units provides redundancy and arguably better total value.

## Who should not buy the Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow

Any user on life-dependent oxygen. Any user without 24/7 caregiver presence. Any user where the two patients have different flow requirements (the fixed-sum 5 LPM architecture cannot simultaneously serve a 4 LPM patient and a 2 LPM patient). Any user in tier-2 cities without a declared service network. Any user with a currently-active prescription, given the machine's Discontinued stock status — buying a discontinued machine for life-critical use is a red flag because spare parts availability degrades rapidly after discontinuation.

## How it compares: Niscomed Dual Flow vs two Keyhub 5 LPM units vs Fitmate 10 LPM Y-split

**Niscomed Dual Flow vs two Keyhub 5 LPM units** — Two Keyhubs total ₹84,480 (₹26,880 more than Niscomed Dual Flow). Two Keyhubs provide redundancy (one breaks, patient two still has oxygen), independent flow-meter settings per patient, combined 10 LPM total capacity (vs Niscomed's 5 LPM total), and Keyhub's CE certification and two declared alarms per unit. The Keyhub configuration is materially better across every clinical dimension. The only Niscomed advantage is lower floor-space footprint (one chassis vs two). Verdict: two Keyhubs win for every clinical scenario except absolute floor-space-constrained installations.

**Niscomed Dual Flow vs Fitmate 10 LPM with Y-split** — Fitmate 10 at ₹1,34,400 (₹76,800 more) provides 10 LPM capacity for Y-split use (each patient can access up to 5 LPM each, or one patient the full 10 LPM). Fitmate has CE certification, loss-of-power and malfunction alarms, and lighter weight at 25.5 kg (vs 25 kg — essentially equivalent). For dual-patient use where one or both may need higher flows periodically, the Fitmate 10 is the correct architecture — it provides flexibility that the Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow's fixed 5 LPM total cannot. Verdict: Fitmate 10 wins if budget allows and patient flow requirements may change.

**Niscomed Dual Flow vs two Oxybliss 5 LPM units** — Two Oxybliss units total ₹72,960 (₹15,360 more than Niscomed Dual Flow). Two Oxybliss units provide redundancy, lower combined noise (40 dB per unit vs Niscomed 50 dB), lower combined power draw (600 W total vs Niscomed 530 W — Niscomed wins marginally on power here), and lighter per-unit weight. Verdict: two Oxybliss units win on redundancy and noise at a modest price premium.

## Indian-market considerations

The Discontinued stock status is the single most important fact about this product. A discontinued medical device in India means:
1. Spare parts supply is capped and will degrade over time as dealer stock depletes.
2. Service contracts typically will not extend beyond whatever existing stock of critical components (sieve beds, compressors, solenoid valves) the importer has on hand.
3. Warranty claims may be honoured against remaining inventory but will transition to "best effort" service as parts run out.
4. Resale value drops sharply — buyers planning to resell after use will lose significantly more on depreciation than they would with a currently-supported model.

For a life-critical medical device, buying a discontinued unit is only defensible when the buyer has a documented three-year parts-and-service contract with the dealer, priced into the purchase. Most Indian dealers will not provide such a contract on discontinued Chinese imports.

CDSCO notification: not declared [CITATION: CDSCO notification database]. No Indian regulatory registration declared.

Power infrastructure: 530 W requires 2 kVA AVR stabiliser (₹5,000-8,000). For dual-patient use an inverter UPS is not optional — a single power cut that exceeds the concentrator's recovery time can desaturate two patients simultaneously. 2 kVA UPS with 30-minute backup is ₹18,000-25,000.

Warranty: typically 1 year from importing distributor. Given discontinued status, extended warranty offers should be treated with extra skepticism.

Service network: no declared national service network, and the discontinued status means even the existing dealer-level service capacity will sunset. For tier-2 city buyers this is particularly problematic.

## Verdict

The Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow is not a machine to buy today. The Discontinued stock status alone rules it out for long-term use. The fundamental design concept — dual-flow outlet for two patients from one machine — is defensible in principle but introduces single-point-of-failure risk that outweighs the benefit. Two separate budget 5 LPM units (Keyhub, Oxybliss) provide redundancy plus better specs for similar money. For patients needing actual dual-patient capacity with flow flexibility, a Fitmate 10 LPM with Y-split is the architecturally correct answer, at roughly ₹77,000 more. The Niscomed 5 LPM Dual Flow sits in a product niche that is better served by every alternative in both directions — up-spec (Fitmate 10) or redundant-dual (two Keyhubs). Score: 4.2 out of 10.
